This is a personal attempt to try and predict progress in AI over the next couple of years, so I can figure out what the best thing to do next looks like.

Currently, there seem to be two major bottlenecks to AI's being useful in the real world.
1. *Continual Learning*: Models get frightingly competent at the end of long conversations, unfortunately, these conversations end. So the model fails to build up experience, and you get to build up frustration.
2. *Long Horizion Tasks*: This seems to be less of a problem in the age of agentic RL, but still seems to be a major problem! Towards the end of conversations, in some cases models can get kind of loopy, acting noticably less competant than they were before. This is a general case of a more pervasive context sensitvity. Having to manage context like this is really annoying, and is a serious damper on model reliablity.

All in all, I think models are actually much more intellegent then they need to be to automate a large fraction of work, they just aren't that good at being agentic. I personally feel like once these two problems are solved, we'll see a massive discontious jump in the value of LLMs.

Online I see two distinct paths forward:
1. A lab rapidly solves these problems and they interally use the agent to develop the next version of the agent and so on, only releasing outside value to generate more capital to feed to the flywheel. Most of the upside of the agents is captured by whoever has A) the model weights, B) the compute to run them. So the lab.
2. These problems remain sticky, however we can schlep past them to get a lot of automation done. The world doesn't rapidly change with one big model release, but it slowly transforms as more and more automation is introduced. Firms develop specialized training systems/models to solve problems across the economy. As change is slower, and secrets and techniques have time to diffuse through the industry, labs are in almost perfect competition with each other, and infrence costs fall then climb back up as providers move towards profitablity. Much of the value is captured by the firms schlepping and integrating the automation throughout the economy.

I think in scenario one, it's probably best to be at the leading lab, which will capture the lions share of the upside. In scenario two it might be worth building your own thing, probably trying to eat up as much automation market share as possible. 

This of course is assuming that we don't all die, but what can you do then?
